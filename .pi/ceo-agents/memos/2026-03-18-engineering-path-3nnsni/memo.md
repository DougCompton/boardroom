## Key Question

What engineering path best compounds capability and leverage over the next several years?

## Board Consensus (Round 1)

The board reached near-unanimous alignment on a single thesis: **AI-native engineering skill alone is a depreciating asset; paired with genuine domain depth, it becomes a compounding one.** Every accepting member independently converged on the same structure—AI fluency as force multiplier, domain expertise as moat. Revenue flagged that pure AI skill reprices toward zero as tooling commoditizes. Product Strategist put an 18-month clock on undifferentiated AI work. Technical Architect stressed owning the *problem semantics*, not just the model integration. Compounder and Moonshot both emphasized the intersection as the only durable position.

## Critical Correction (Round 2)

The Contrarian's deferral is the most important signal in this session. The objection: *every engineer will claim a domain anchor within 18 months, recreating commoditization one layer up.* This is not a rejection of the thesis—it's a quality gate on execution. A shallow domain claim is no moat at all. Moonshot concretized the fix: the domain must be **high-stakes** (healthcare, legal, financial infrastructure) where expertise is genuinely hard to acquire, hard to verify, and carries real consequences for getting it wrong. That difficulty *is* the barrier.

## CEO Decision

**Commit to AI-native systems engineering anchored in a single high-stakes domain where expertise is hard-won and verifiable.**

The reasoning is straightforward:

1. AI fluency is necessary but insufficient—it is becoming baseline.
2. Domain depth is the only durable differentiator, but only if the domain has genuine acquisition cost (years, not weeks).
3. The intersection compounds: each project deepens domain authority *and* sharpens AI-native craft simultaneously.
4. Pick one domain. Go deep. Do not hedge across verticals.

## The Formula

> **Hard-to-acquire domain expertise × AI-native engineering fluency = compounding leverage that resists commoditization.**

The key word is *hard-to-acquire*. If the domain knowledge can be absorbed from a weekend course or a documentation site, it is not a moat.

## Traps to Avoid

1. **The Shallow Anchor Trap** — Claiming a domain without doing the painful, slow work of genuine depth. The Contrarian is right: a label is not a moat.
2. **The Tool Identity Trap** — Defining yourself by AI tooling rather than outcomes. Tools change quarterly; domain authority persists.
3. **The Breadth Trap** — Spreading across multiple verticals to "keep options open." This guarantees you never compound in any of them.
4. **The Build-in-Isolation Trap** — Developing domain expertise without shipping artifacts that are visible, explainable, and credibility-building.
5. **The Premature Optimization Trap** — Over-engineering AI integrations before the domain problem is deeply understood.

## Litmus Tests

- **On track:** You can explain a domain problem to a specialist and they take you seriously. Your AI-native work solves problems others in the domain cannot frame correctly.
- **Drifting:** You're building AI demos without domain users. Your work is impressive technically but interchangeable with anyone else using the same stack.
- **Red flag:** You cannot name the specific domain. You describe your work in terms of tools rather than problems solved.

## Conditions / Risks

- **Domain selection is load-bearing.** A wrong pick (too shallow, too crowded, declining market) undermines the entire strategy. This choice deserves its own deliberation.
- **The Contrarian's 18-month clock is real.** If domain depth isn't demonstrably hard-won within that window, the position collapses to commodity.
- **Capacity constraint applies.** Deep domain work and AI-native engineering both demand significant learning bandwidth. Sequencing matters—domain understanding first, AI application second.

## Immediate Next Moves

1. **Select the domain.** Evaluate 2–3 candidate verticals against three criteria: genuine acquisition difficulty, market demand for AI-native solutions, and existing proximity or advantage. Decide within two weeks.
2. **Identify a domain entry point.** Find one real, scoped problem in the chosen domain that requires both domain understanding and AI-native craft. Ship something small and visible.
3. **Build credibility artifacts.** Every project should produce explainable, durable work product—not just code, but writing, case studies, or tools that demonstrate domain authority.
4. **Set the 90-day checkpoint.** At 90 days, apply the litmus tests above. If the domain choice isn't compounding, reassess before sunk cost sets in.

## Recommendation Rationale

This decision is not novel—every board member saw the same structure independently, which is itself a strong signal. The only refinement worth making was the Contrarian's: *depth must be real, not cosmetic.* The strategy is simple. The execution is hard. That's the point—if it were easy, it wouldn't be a moat.
